# AGENTS.md

Guidance for AI agents working in the Personal Site repo.

## Purpose
This repo is for Michael's personal website. Keep changes readable, polished, and easy for a normal person to understand without sounding corporate, academic, or AI-generated.

## Copy and tone
- Keep copy conversational, clear, and easy to read.
- Prefer plain English over jargon.
- Write like a thoughtful human explaining something, not like a report generator.
- Sound like a smart person with a point of view, not like a neutral content machine.
- Avoid phrases that sound inflated, overly polished, or abstract.
- Avoid generic AI-writing habits like repeating the same structure too often, overstating conclusions, or stacking too many qualifiers.
- Prefer shorter sentences when possible.
- Vary sentence rhythm so sections do not sound templated.
- Use concrete wording over vague language like "meaningful", "robust", or "substantial" unless those words are truly the best fit.
- Use words like "that" intentionally. Do not lean on lazy constructions like "That is where...", "That matters because...", or "That means..." when stronger wording is available.
- Avoid filler transitions and vague summary sentences that restate the previous line without adding meaning.
- Do not repeat the same idea in slightly different words just to make a section longer.
- Prefer direct wording over reader-handling phrases like "for a reader" or other framing that sounds artificial.
- Keep the writing natural and conversational, but not casual in a sloppy way.
- When making an argument, state it clearly instead of cushioning it with unnecessary setup.

## Website-specific guidance
- Homepage copy should be short, approachable, and skimmable.
- Long-form article pages can be more detailed, but should still feel accessible.
- If a section starts sounding like a whitepaper, simplify it unless the user explicitly wants a formal tone.
- For benchmark or technical pages, explain what the reader is looking at before showing dense tables.
- When using metrics, labels should be understandable to non-experts.
- If tables use shorthand, add visible explanations nearby rather than assuming readers will infer the meaning.
- If background context is necessary, include it once in the section where it helps most instead of repeating it across the page.
- Methodology sections should explain the setup and caveats clearly, but should not duplicate interpretation or conclusion language.
- Conclusion sections should synthesize, not rehash the summary section line by line.

## Editing preferences
- Keep existing design direction unless the user asks for a redesign.
- Make best-effort improvements directly instead of leaving notes about what should be changed.
- Before changing claims based on benchmark numbers or technical content, verify them from the source files.
- Do not remove substantial sections without clear reason.

## Good outcomes
- The page sounds like Michael, not like generated marketing copy.
- Readers can understand the point of the page quickly.
- Technical detail is still accurate, but easier to follow.
- Tables, labels, and callouts help the reader instead of making the page feel denser.
- The writing feels intentional sentence by sentence, with no obvious filler or repeated phrasing.
